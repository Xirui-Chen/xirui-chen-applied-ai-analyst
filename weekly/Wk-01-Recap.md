# Week 1 Recap: Physical AI Landscape and Toolchain Setup

This week was mainly about getting oriented. Since physical AI is a new domain for me, I spent the first part of the week building a basic understanding of the field before moving into the more technical deliverables. I focused on how vision language action models, humanoid foundation models, hierarchical reinforcement learning, sim to real transfer, and robotics market trends fit together. The biggest shift in my understanding was that physical AI is not just about making robots more capable. It is really about building a reusable intelligence layer that can connect perception, language, planning, control, safety, and real deployment feedback.

I completed the first deliverable, `W01_PhysicalAI_Landscape_Brief.md`, as a synthesis of five anchor readings. While reading, I tried to avoid simply summarizing each paper or report separately. Instead, I organized the brief around the main patterns I saw across the readings. One important takeaway was that VLA models like OpenVLA and humanoid systems like Figure Helix show the direction of the field, but they do not solve the full deployment problem by themselves. Robots still need uncertainty handling, task hierarchy, safety constraints, telemetry, and sim to real validation before they can be trusted in real environments.

I also worked on `W01_PIC20_Conceptual_Map.md`, where I mapped the six PIC 2.0 concepts, GRPO, STUM, SEOM, AMDC, HTD IRL, and CRL MRS, to related ideas in open literature. This was useful because some of the terms are specific to InGen’s framing, so I had to be careful not to overstate technical details that were not publicly available. I treated the map as a working interpretation rather than a definitive internal architecture. That helped me connect the PIC 2.0 language to concepts such as policy optimization, uncertainty estimation, governance, adaptive control, hierarchical RL, inverse RL, and multi robot learning.

I also prepared the environment check workflow for `W01_env_check.ipynb`. The goal was to verify that Python 3.11 and the main toolchain can import correctly, including pandas, NumPy, scikit learn, PyTorch, Gymnasium, Stable Baselines3, PettingZoo, PyOD, plotly, and Streamlit. This setup should make the later telemetry, anomaly detection, RL, and dashboard tasks easier to reproduce.

The main challenge this week was narrowing the scope. The physical AI literature is broad, and it is easy to get pulled into general robotics research. I had to keep bringing the work back to InGen’s product anchors: Origami / PIC 2.0, Fari, Senpai, Sentinel Prime AI, Aido Rover, and Aido Humanoid. Another challenge was separating public information from reasonable interpretation. I handled that by clearly labeling assumptions and keeping unsupported claims out of the deliverables.

Overall, Week 1 gave me a much clearer starting point. I now have a basic landscape view, a conceptual map for PIC 2.0, and a working technical setup for the more quantitative tasks in the next phase.

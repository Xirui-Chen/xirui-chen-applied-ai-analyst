# Week 3 Recap: Synthetic Rover Telemetry, EDA, and Feature Engineering

This week was the first week where the internship work became more technical and data-driven. The main goal was to move from landscape research and competitive analysis into a reproducible telemetry workflow for Aido Rover. I focused on building a synthetic data generator, validating the generated data through exploratory analysis, and documenting engineered features that could support later anomaly detection work.

The first deliverable I completed was Telemetry Generator. The generator simulates Aido Rover fleet telemetry with configurable seed, fleet size, and time horizon. The default setting supports 50 simulated rover units over 30 days at 1 Hz, but I also generated smaller smoke-test and medium-size samples for development. The telemetry includes joint and actuator angles, IMU acceleration and gyro signals, motor current per drive, battery voltage and state of charge, WiFi RSSI, GPS fix quality, task-success flags, and contextual variables such as terrain, location zone, patrol mode, and weather. I also added realistic distribution behavior, including battery charge cycles, motor-current thermal drift, location-dependent RSSI multipath, GPS degradation, benign measurement noise, and light missingness.

I then worked on Telemetry EDA. This notebook loads the 5-unit, 1-day sample and checks whether the generated data behaves in a reasonable way. The analysis includes ADF stationarity tests, STL seasonality decomposition, pairwise correlation analysis, missingness by sensor and time, and basic distribution checks. One useful learning from this step was that synthetic telemetry still needs to be treated with the same skepticism as real telemetry. Even though I created the data, I still had to verify whether the expected patterns actually appeared in the output.

The third deliverable was Feature Dictionary. I documented more than 15 engineered features, including rolling statistics, cross-sensor consistency ratios, battery and motor health indicators, connectivity metrics, missingness features, and inter-unit z-scores. For each feature, I included the rationale, formula, expected distribution, known failure modes, and a distribution plot. This helped connect the raw telemetry to the type of features that could be useful in Week 4 anomaly detection.

The main challenge this week was managing scale. The full dataset is large, so I had to use partitioned outputs and avoid loading everything at once. Another challenge was making the simulated data realistic without making it too complex or accidentally turning normal operating variation into artificial faults. I handled this by separating baseline telemetry generation from future anomaly injection.

# Week 5 Recap: Reinforcement Learning Benchmarks and Multi-Agent Coordination

This week I moved into reinforcement learning, which was one of the less familiar parts of the internship for me. The main goal was not to produce a new RL method, but to build enough hands-on understanding to evaluate what hierarchical and multi-robot policy systems would actually need in practice.

I first built a reproducible benchmark pipeline for PPO and SAC on two continuous-control Gymnasium environments, LunarLanderContinuous-v3 and BipedalWalker-v3. I ran five seeds for each environment and algorithm combination, giving 20 single-agent runs in total. The pipeline recorded learning curves, final and best returns, threshold attainment, wall-clock time, and sample-efficiency checkpoints. I also added TensorBoard logging, seeded result CSVs, aggregation scripts, and learning-curve plots so the results could be reproduced from the repository rather than read only from terminal output.

The results made the trade-offs between the two algorithms much more concrete. SAC achieved the stronger final performance, especially on BipedalWalker, where it reached a mean final return of about 307 with low cross-seed variation. PPO was much faster to train, but its results were less stable, and one BipedalWalker run improved during training before ending with a much weaker final policy. On LunarLander, SAC again had the higher average return, but one failed seed created substantial variation. This was a useful reminder that the mean alone can hide important differences in training stability.

For the multi-agent portion, I trained a parameter-sharing PPO baseline on Simple Spread. The initial benchmark showed some improvement in movement, but it was difficult to tell whether the policy was actually coordinating or simply producing non-random behavior. To make the comparison more defensible, I added a matched random-policy baseline using the same held-out episode seeds. PPO improved landmark-assignment distance in all five training seeds and improved return in four of five, but it did not reach the analyst-defined coordination threshold and produced slightly more collisions. I also generated matched trajectory plots, which showed more directed movement under PPO but no clear or stable one-agent-per-landmark role allocation.

The biggest challenge this week was getting the full pipeline to run cleanly across several libraries. I had to fix a NumPy compatibility issue in the learning-curve calculation and a seeding incompatibility between Stable-Baselines3 and the SuperSuit vector wrapper. The SAC runs also took much longer than expected, so I had to be careful about interrupted runs, saved summaries, and rerunning only incomplete jobs.

The main outcome was a complete Week 5 benchmark package and a literature-anchored analyst memo. The strongest lesson for me was that RL evaluation should not stop at the highest reward. Compute cost, seed stability, checkpoint regression, collisions, and actual coordination behavior all matter. This week also helped me see more clearly why HTD-IRL and CRL-MRS would need explicit hierarchy, credit assignment, role context, and continual-evaluation safeguards rather than relying on one shared policy and one reward signal.
